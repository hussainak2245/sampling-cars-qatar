From Sirens to Social Media: Qatar University Students & the Iranian Attacks

Phase 1: Questionnaire Development and Peer Validation

Author

Hussain Ahamed

Published

April 29, 2026

Introduction

Background

Recent geopolitical tensions in the Middle East led to missile and drone attacks targeting strategic locations in the Gulf region, including Qatar. These events triggered widespread public concern, with emergency alert systems activated across the country. Residents received repeated mobile alerts, while universities transitioned to remote learning and authorities enforced strict safety directives.

For some university students, this period was marked not only by physical disruptions but also by psychological stress. The sounds of missile interceptions, uncertainty about safety, and continuous exposure to news and social media created a complex information and emotional environment. In particular, platforms such as Al Jazeera, WhatsApp, Instagram, and Twitter played a critical role in shaping perceptions, often mixing reliable updates with misinformation.

Understanding how students perceived the threat, responded to emergency alerts, consumed media, and complied with safety measures is essential. These insights can inform future crisis communication strategies, improve emergency alert systems, and enhance public safety policies.

Objective

The objective of this project is to develop a questionnaire and validate it to assess university students’ perceptions and behaviors during the Iranian attacks in Qatar, focusing on four key constructs:

  • Emotional Threat Perception
  • Emergency Alert System Perception
  • News & Media Perception
  • Behavioral Compliance

Target Population and Sampling Design

Target Population

The target population for this study is undergraduate university students in Qatar. The study focuses on students enrolled in major institutions, including:

  • Qatar University (QU)
  • University of Doha for Science and Technology (UDST)
  • Qatar Foundation (Education City universities)

Based on available institutional data, the approximate population sizes are:

Institution Approximate Students
Qatar University ~23,000
UDST ~8,000
Qatar Foundation Universities ~4,000
Total (Estimated) ~35,000

This population represents a substantial portion of the university student body in Qatar and is considered a reasonable approximation for the purpose of this study.

Population Characteristics

Available datasets suggest that the student population is diverse, particularly in terms of:

  • Nationality (Qatari vs. non-Qatari residents)
  • Institutional affiliation

This indicates that student perceptions may vary across these groups, and therefore, a sampling design that ensures representation across key subgroups is desirable.

Proposed Sampling Method

In an ideal setting, Simple Random Sampling (SRS) would be used to obtain a representative sample. However, this would require access to a complete sampling frame (i.e., a full list of all university students in Qatar), which is not available.

Therefore, for this study, a quota-based sampling approach is proposed.

Rationale

  • Ensures inclusion of students from different universities
  • Accounts for variation in population sizes across institutions
  • Provides more balanced representation than pure convenience sampling

Proposed Quota Structure

To approximate the population distribution, quotas are defined based on institution:

Institution Approximate Share Proposed Sample (n = 100)
Qatar University ~66% 66
UDST ~23% 23
Qatar Foundation ~11% 11

In addition, an approximate balance across nationality (Qatari vs. resident) is considered to ensure diversity in responses.

Within-Quota Sampling

Within each quota group, responses would be collected using convenience sampling, such as:

  • Online survey distribution
  • Student networks and peer sharing

Thus, the overall design can be described as a:

Quota-based non-probability sampling approach with convenience selection within groups

Sample Size Consideration

The theoretical sample size for estimating proportions is given by: \[ n_0 = \frac{Z^2 p(1-p)}{e^2} \]

Using: - \(Z = 1.96\) (95% confidence level)
- \(p = 0.5\)
- \(e = 0.10\)

This yields: \[ n_0 = \frac{(1.96)^2 \cdot 0.5 \cdot 0.5}{(0.10)^2} \approx 96 \]

However, for the purpose of this course project, a target sample size of:

\[ n =100\]

is considered acceptable. This implies a higher margin of error (approximately 14%), and therefore, results should be interpreted as exploratory rather than conclusive.

Limitations of the Proposed Design

  • Non-probability sampling limits statistical generalizability
  • Convenience sampling may introduce response bias
  • Small sample size increases variability

Despite these limitations, the proposed design provides a practical and structured approach suitable for exploratory analysis within a course setting.

9. Summary

This study proposes a quota-based sampling design informed by approximate population structure, balancing statistical reasoning with practical feasibility. The approach ensures representation across key groups while remaining appropriate for a course-based research project.

Peer Validation Analysis

Overview

The questionnaire was evaluated by peer groups to assess the clarity and relevance of each item. Each question was rated on a scale from 1 (low) to 5 (high).

A total of 4 peer reviewers evaluated all items across the four constructs.

Items Removed or Revised After Peer Validation

No item was highly irrelevant overall; however, the lowest-scoring or unclear item in each affected construct was revised. This keeps the required 5 Likert items per construct while improving validity.
